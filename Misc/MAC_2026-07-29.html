The Magic Circle

Baby Center Stage - Chapter 5/9

The train sped up, blending the wet foliage outside into a silvery green oil painting as the boxcar rocked gently. The sound of the wheels was oddly comforting; the motion and directionality were reassuring – we’re headed somewhere. Jax sat on the lip of the open metal door, gazing out into the light and swinging his feet over the edge whimsically; he imagined himself running alongside the train, darting around trees and cutting through bushes in a mad scramble faster than any human could run.

 Raven sat down next to him, their legs crossed on the dark wooden floor. He glanced at them, then looked back out into the trees. When he checked back after a moment, they were still regarding him, brown eyes narrowed. “…what,” he said flatly.

 “You’re new to this.” It wasn’t said like a question, but they waited afterwards as though it was.

 Jax sighed. “Yeah, I guess.”

 They looked at him a moment longer, then turned and rustled through their bag. Jax’s eyes darted over them, trying not to seem too interested. After a few moments, they turned back around and held out a piece of beef jerky. “Here; this is the good stuff.”

 “No thanks,” Jax said, looking away. As soon as he said it his stomach audibly churned, as though to rat him out.

 “Tch,” Raven said, “y’know, Jax, the first rule of the road is to never turn down food. Don’t worry. I don’t bite.”

 Wren piped up from the corner, “not often, anyway!”

 Raven smiled, raising the dried beef once again in his direction.

 Jax cracked a small grin and took the jerky, giving only a tiny glance in Raven’s direction. He pulled a piece off with his teeth – it was tough, but not nearly so tough as he expected. He wasn’t sure if it was just because he was so hungry, but it tasted delicious, like it sated some deep and wild craving inside of him. He closed his eyes; it was all he could do to not audibly moan at the sensation. “Thanks,” he said finally.

 “You bet,” Raven said out of the corner of their mouth. “When we get to N’Orleans, I want you to come with us for a while; we’ll show you the ropes.”

 Jax looked sideways at them. “Yeah? You think I need your help?”

 “I know you do,” Raven said plainly. “Look, it’s not easy out here. We have to look after each other – I can’t let you venture out on your own in good conscience; you’d get eaten alive. Hey, “they put their hand on his shoulder, and he turned his head to meet their eyes. “Jax, listen. I can tell you’re going through it right now. It’s okay. Let us help you. We’ll all be this together, yeah?”

 “Yeah!” Wren chimed in.

 Jax felt tears welling up, and with them rose fear. His heart was pounding. It took focus, but he willed the tears back down into himself and managed a nod. “Okay.”

 Raven never broke eye contact; they were looking deep into him, and there was something they were reflecting back. Gentleness, seriousness, and maybe some kind of understanding. “Okay,” they echoed.

 Jax lay in his room, staring up at the smattering of luminescent plastic stars on his ceiling. Or well, they weren’t plastic – nothing was anything in here. A pang of anxiety pierced his guts, and he rolled over on the bed in pain. He was beginning to grasp the logic of this reality, but he didn’t really believe in it yet. It was all just too bizarre, and so far beyond the realm of his knowledge. He breathed shakily, remembering a simple sentence Ragatha had said earlier: “we don’t know how to leave.” It was less the specific words and more how she said it – she clearly believed she was here forever. And that idea… Jax winced, then rolled over to his other side.

 There was a knock at the door. “Jax?” It was Ragatha’s voice. He said nothing. “Jax, we totally understand if you need space right now. This is… well, it’s a lot. We all know what it’s like; we’ve all been through it. I just, want you to know that you’re not alone, okay?”

 “We’re all here, Jax, just right beyond the door,” it was Ribbit’s voice. “If you need anything, we are all right here for you.”

 “Me too, man. And Kinger,” said Kaufmo, his deep voice contrasting sharply with the others.

“And Kaufmo. And Ribbit and Ragatha. Oh, and me!” said Kinger meanderingly.

 Jax let out a long breath. Something in him didn’t want to admit it, but he did feel a little better knowing that they were just outside his door. It didn’t change a thing, he was still stuck here, but it did affect him. “Okay,” he said, trying to keep his voice from wavering. Two more deep breaths, then he closed his eyes and tried to sleep.

 Jax awoke with a start, gasping for breath. Again, it took him several seconds to become oriented to where he was – a common occurrence these days, it seemed. The room hadn’t changed, and there was no indication how much time had passed; the only light was artificial, of course it was, and came from beneath the door to the main hallway. Jax rubbed his eyes; feeling his three-fingered gloved hands against his face was still quite strange. He noticed a lamp on the end table by his head and pulled the string. He blinked in surprise; the lamp cover was pink, with flowers? That couldn’t be right for his room. He scanned the rest of the space in the new, gentle light; the walls were a striped pink with rainbows, clouds, and shooting stars; the floor was a bright, joyful blue with an area rug in white, pink and purple; a plush seal sat roundly upon a wooden desk; and, most interestingly, there was a bookshelf full of indulgent supernatural young adult novels and romance – many of the same books he’d checked out from the library but kept hidden from his parents.

 “Well, this is gay,” he said quietly. Okay, Caine clearly made a mistake. Nobody can see this. Asking the ringmaster for changes crossed his mind, but he let the thought slip away. He’d keep it as is, for now. Mattered not at all.

 Jax opened his door quietly and stepped out into the hall, closing it quickly behind him to deny the others a view inside. He looked down and saw only Ribbit; she was slouched against the wall and fast asleep, snoring gently. His heart warmed, then sank – how long had he been in there? He knelt down and placed a hand on her shoulder. “Hey. Ribbit, hey.”

 She stirred, her frog eyes sliding open. “Eh? Oh, Jax! Hey!” She stood up and stretched; he stood alongside her and couldn’t help but notice just how much taller than her he was, not even counting the ears. That made him feel, bad? Do I really feel bad about that? We’re cartoons. He chased the thought away.

 “You uh, you didn’t have to stay out here. I’m okay,” he said, and didn’t even believe himself. He had to try harder – he crossed his arms and leaned against the wall, “’s what I do, y’know? I just roll with the punches. Even if the punches are from a spring-loaded looney-toons boxing glove,” he forced a smile.

 Ribbit looked up at him and grinned weakly. “That’s good to hear, Jax. Back when I arrived here, about a year ago, I definitely wasn’t okay for like, a while.” She leaned on the wall, mirroring his posture. “It is amazing what you can get used to, though. I’m just glad to have the others. We get each other through.”

 “Heh. One big family, huh?” Jax said, less sarcastically than he intended.

 Ribbit righted herself. “You joke, but, yeah, pretty much. You’re welcome here, you know – you’re one of us now.” She put her hand on his arm, and he felt himself blush slightly. “Come on, the others went to the café. I’ll show you!” He followed her across the cavernous space, a mix of emotions sloshing around in his gut. Nothing a little food won’t fix.

 “Are you sure about this?” Jax asked as they strode across the parking lot, trying to hide the apprehension in his voice. “Wouldn’t it be easier to just go to the food bank?”

 “It would be easier,” Wren said, not turning around. “But we’re not doing this because it’s easier, or safer.”

 “Why, then?” Jax said, feeling as though he’d just asked a stupid question.

 “Because fuck ‘em,” said Raven, shooting Jax a toothy smirk. Their face had a harshness to it, their eyes stern with passion. “These bastards would rather throw away overstock than feed their neighbors. Their employees are on food stamps. They undercut local stores and then absorb them into their soulless hegemony. Taking anything from these suits is a win for humanity; they’re lucky we don’t just burn the place down.”

 That filled Jax with excitement. He’d always felt the call of rebellion, but he’d never really gotten to answer it like this. This is going to be fun. “Yeah, fuck ‘em!” he said with glee, and the trio entered the sliding doors into the fluorescent lights, excessive air conditioning, and dated pop radio that Jax knew all too well.

 “Y’know, I used to work at a place like this,” Jax said as he and Raven walked down the canned goods aisle. “Kinda weird to be back in this environment.”

 “Damn, that must’ve sucked ass,” they said. “Too bad you’re not still there; you could’ve been our man on the inside.” Raven hesitated, and a beat passed between the two. Jax realized that she – h-he, had flinched at the comment.

 He shoved the conversation forward, “Y-Yeah, too bad, I guess. Still, my experience will come in useful,” he said as he scanned the ceiling above them. “I think we’re in the clear here – you ready?”

 “Always,” said Raven, unzipping their bag. “Mm, Bartlett pears; don’t mind if I do!” they swept a can in. Jax followed suit, grabbing a bit of this and a bit of that.

 As Jax and Ribbit walked along, they passed an ornate mirror fastened into the wall. The rabbit froze in his tracks, staring intently at his new reflection. Ribbit’s brow furrowed slightly with worry, and she followed him over to get a better look.

 Jax reached a gloved hand up to touch his cheek. The black rectangular pools in his yellow sclera narrowed as he directed them over his form. He was ridiculously purple, ridiculously tall, and wearing a ridiculous pair of orange and yellow overalls to match his gloves. “I look… stupid.” His face fell – this was a familiar feeling.

 Ribbit put on her best smile. “Heh, I remember that feeling. It goes away eventually.” Jax looked back at her, and she saw something in his eyes that changed her expression in an indescribable way. Her head tilted slightly, sympathetically. “Come on, let’s go.”

 “Oh, hey Jax!” said Kaufmo as he and Ribbit walked into the café. “Saved you a spot,” he gestured at the barstool next to him. Ragatha and Kinger were seated at a table off to the side, and the ragdoll waved at them.

 “Thanks,” Jax sad as he sat down and picked up the menu. For all the whimsy and outlandishness of the circus, the offerings at the Café du Cirque were shockingly, normal? Things like “Croque Madame” and “Chocolate Éclair.” Seeing something so familiar was refreshing, and Jax allowed the muscles in his shoulders to relax a bit.

 “The hot chocolate is pretty good!” Ribbit offered, sitting down on the other side of him. “Kaufmo and I made it kind of a tradition to come up here and get it every week.”

 Kaufmo raised the glass he was already drinking. “Can’t even tell it’s not real,” he said happily, taking a swig.

 Jax looked at the menu again, and thought for a moment. “Wait so, I mean, we’re cartoon characters, right? How does us eating work – do we have a digestive system? H🤬, I’m not even sure I have anything behind these, teeth?” Jax pawed at his mouth, which was a strikingly uncomfortable sensation. He felt himself struggling to breathe a little again – this was all so d🤬 claustrophobic.

 “Hey. Relax,” said Kaufmo, putting his hand on Jax’s back. “Deep breaths, one thing at a time. It’s okay.”

 “Yeah, it’s simple. Cartoon logic, right? You can just, kinda eat. Don’t worry about it too much; not everything in the circus fits together all the time. Roll with the punches, remember?” Ribbit pitched in reassuringly.

 “Y-Yeah, okay.” Jax said, swallowing. “I guess I should try that hot chocolate, huh?” Ribbit smiled warmly.

 Suddenly, Caine burst through the café doors, eyes wide with excitement. “Hello my eager epicureans! Who’s ready for an ADVENTURE?”

 “Omg, meeee!” chirped bubble, helpfully.

 “Eeeh,” sighed Ragatha.

 “Jax! I see you’ve taken a liking to our wonderful bubble-powered cuisine! How does a DELICIOUS culinary adventure sound for your maiden voyage?” A trail of sparks fell from Caine’s hand as he dramatically waved it through the air.

 “All my food has Bluetooth,” muttered bubble.

 “I uh, adventure?” Asked Jax, genuinely confused.

 Kaufmo sighed. “Caine makes these little games for us to help pass the time. Some are pretty alright, I guess.”

 “Pretty alright? My adventures will blow your rabbit tail clean off; I guarantee it! Ha ha!” Caine shouted.

 “I brought peroxide for the tail blood,” said bubble.

 “She doesn’t mean it. Anyway, away we go!” Caine waved his staff and a blue portal appeared in the middle of the café.

 “Are we really doing this?” Jax asked as the others walked toward the opening. “We just spend all our time playing little games with each other?” He felt his ears droop with his mood – that’s funny that they do that.

 “Come on Jax, give it a shot. Surely there are worse fates than playing with your friends for all eternity,” Ribbit said, holding out her hand.

 “Tsh, that’s kinda cringe, Ribbit,” Jax said, but he was smiling. He took her hand.
