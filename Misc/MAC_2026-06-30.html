Colossus of Roads [Excerpt]

Baby Center Stage - Chapter 4/9

It was overcast – a gentle ambient silver light hung in the air as thick as the humidity; they dripped down together from leaves high above in gentle rain, dotting on James’s nose. The smell of soil filled the air he slinked through, mixing with the darkly roasted gas station coffee he was sipping. He’d been able to pawn several of the items from the office building for a surprisingly substantive sum – well, substantive in the context of day-to-day survival – and he’d been able to pick up some basic supplies like a sleeping bag, water bottle, and a small assortment of snacks.

 Thunder cracked overhead and the rain began to open up, plopping loudly into his motor-oil coffee. Ugh. Well, at least it’s not raining! he mused drearily as he wandered down the path through the trees. He was as aimless as the moment he’d taken off on foot from his mother’s house, but he felt more at home with it today. He’d never had much of a purpose anyway – this wasn’t really meaningfully different, and he could still have some fun, probably. He picked up a large branch and swung it through the air, sending a smattering of droplets deep into the underbrush.

 The falling rain shone with a brilliant blue, then red, then blue. An approaching siren silenced the cicadas as James’s heart plunged into his stomach – he dropped to a crouch. Shit! Are they after me? Does that mean- he killed the thought. He was too close to the road; it was time to run. James took off perpendicular to the street, smashing through jimsonweed, poison oak, and thorny bushes that caught him with their prickles and soaked him with their dampness – it wasn’t a quiet egress. James’s teeth clenched as he ran, both from the pain and the fear.

 After several minutes of running, he realized that he could no longer hear the siren. He stopped to breathe, looking around wildly. Nothing. Once his heart quieted, he inhaled and held it, listening. There was the rustling of the treetops in the wind, the constant patter of the rain, a low chorus of insect hums, and the distant rumbling of thunder. No humans – no cops. They must not have seen him. He let out the breath in relief, only to realize that he had no idea where he was. It was all forest around him, unmanaged and wild. A wet fog had begun to roll in; he could see nothing manmade in any direction.

 “Well, out of the frying pan,” he intoned melodically. He was too relieved about the lack of hot pursuit to let the isolating dread looming behind the fog close in on him just yet. Only one thing to do. He picked a direction and walked.

 The crunching of damp foliage under his shoes was gradually accompanied by the low whine of metal on metal and the distant churning of a powerful machine. James perked up, curious, and walked in the direction of the sound. Eventually he emerged from the brush to see a narrowly cleared swath for the slow-moving train that was rumbling along. Watching the gleaming boxcars pass one after another reminded James of an impossibly long segmented insect crawling mechanically along a cracked wall.

 There was art emblazoned onto the carapace of this great creature; the flamboyant smatterings of color, abstract shapes, and sharply angled lines entranced James – he wondered how many people had contributed to this expansive, living piece, and for how many years it had been built upon.

 As the train neared its end, James noticed a sketch that was different from the others. It was strikingly simple: one color and only a few thin strokes outlined the visage of a stern-brimmed cowboy with a matchingly stern-brimmed hat facing, determined, in the direction of the train’s motion. He held a pipe between his lips which led a long line of smoke behind him as he moved across the Earth. Beneath, there was a phrase: “Remembrance of Ideas.” Huh.

 The boxcar sporting the cryptic cowboy had its metal door partially open; the gray light didn’t much penetrate the interior, but something about it beckoned. It looked cool, and dry, and very much like an exit. But beyond that, James felt that its door was somehow left open for him. He jogged over, tossed up his bag, and hopped in.

 “Woah, woah!” a voice deep in the boxcar’s darkness called out as James found his footing. His eyes adjusted slowly, and two figures faded into view. They were a scrappy looking duo with old and patched clothes, unkept hair, and a multitude of belts, tools and backpacks about them. The one who spoke had a particularly stern look and a hand firmly on their belt, over some kind of sheath. Their light brown skin paled at their knuckles with the strength of their grip. “You gonna be cool?” they demanded.

 James blinked and held up his hands in front of him. “Y-yeah, I’m cool.” He didn’t get the impression he was actually in serious danger, but he was definitely startled. His mind raced for a way to diffuse the situation. “Cool as a cucumber. Cool like Fonzie.” He smiled toothily.

 The duo seemed to relax a little. As James’s eyes adjusted he could see them a little more clearly. They both looked to be a bit younger than him, perhaps in their late teens. He couldn’t get a read on the gender of the one who’d spoken, but the other was most probably a guy – he had a patchy beard and long, brown hair that flowed over his heavyset shoulders. When the two of them stood James noted he was considerably taller than both of them. “You got a name, stranger?” the short-haired, lither one asked.

 It was a surprisingly good question. He didn’t think he liked James much more than Leeroy, but he didn’t know what else to use. “I, uh, I do. But I don’t just tell anybody who asks,” he rolled his hand forward to accentuate his point. “Who’re you guys?”

 Something flashed in the eyes of the talkative one, and they tilted their head slightly. “Call me Raven. This is Wren,” they gestured to their partner, who smiled warmly. “We’re from Calexico, headed through to N’Orleans.”

 Those names intrigued him – they were clearly chosen, and they fit the two of them quite well. Could he make up a name like that? He wouldn’t know where to begin; it seemed like such a big decision. Then again, who cares? I don’t know these two and will probably never see them again. What the hell. “I’m Ja– “ He started, before realizing he hadn’t actually thought of anything yet. Panicking, he cut off the sound, “x? Jax.” He was surprised, hearing it – it wasn’t bad. It was actually pretty nice – Jax. Jax. He felt his cheeks warm, and his “please don’t stab me” smile melted into something a little more genuine.
