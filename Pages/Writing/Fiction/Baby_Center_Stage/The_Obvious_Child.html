The Obvious Child

Baby Center Stage - Chapter 6/9

 Jax wasn’t sure how much time had passed; life in the circus tended to blur together, despite how eventful Caine’s adventures could be. He hadn’t gotten used to the unrelentingly neon color grade of everything, nor had he really settled into his body, but he had to admit he was acclimating to this new life. The other circus members were all really nice, but he felt he could really call Ribbit and Kaufmo his friends. He’d been able to learn bits and pieces of their lives in the quiet moments between adventures or during their more relaxed outings. Kaufmo was a bank teller who did stand-up comedy at local open mics, while Ribbit was a digital accessibility consultant who played the violin at the Louisiana Renaissance Festival. Between them, ironically, it was Ribbit who really made him laugh. Working the faire had given her some wild stories.

 Jax hadn’t talked too much about his past with anyone in the circus. What did it matter? He was a retail worker, and then he was homeless – it really wasn’t that interesting. It’s also entirely irrelevant, now. Like it didn’t happen. Why dwell on it? There was a measure of relief in leaving it behind.

 “Hey dumb-a🤬,” Ribbit taunted from her seat as Jax strolled into the common area and lay down across his signature purple couch.

 He smiled and retorted melodically, “sup, moron. Has your ego recovered from your crushing defeat in today’s adventure?”

 “Pssh, I have no shame in that loss. In fact, you should be ashamed I did so well – you let a frog half your size take the ball from you three times.” Her eyes glimmered with playful antagonism.

 “Yeah yeah yeah, I’m sure you won’t hold that over me forever or anything.” He sighed contentedly, “That is one nice thing about the circus – we can just play sports like it’s no big deal.”

 Ribbit giggled. “What, you mean you weren’t a star athlete out there?”

 “Don’t make me laugh. Do I look like a sporting type to you?”

 “You look like a rabbit, Jax.”

 “Touché.”

 They sat quietly together in the common area, contentedly enjoying each other’s company. Jax closed his eyes, and it struck him for the hundredth time just how quiet the circus really was. There were none of the usual ambient sounds of the real world – no AC, no lighting, no cars – ironically nothing electrical at all. Sadly, there was also no birdsong, wind, rain, or bug noise; that could be depressing at times, but in moments like this the utter quiet was relaxing. It led her into a meditative state – she imagined herself floating in perfect stillness on the ocean; the water rippling gently between her toes.

 “Hey… Jax,” Ribbit said, gently.

 Jax didn’t even open her eyes. “Yeah, Ribbit?” Her voice flowed easily, as gentle and natural as the tide.

 “Do you want to come by my room for a bit? I’ve got something to show you.”

 She stood from the water, opening an eye. Ribbit’s face shone with affection, gleaming and fragile as china.

 “Let there be light!” exclaimed Wren excitedly, raising his hands as smoke began to rise from the makeshift fire pit.

 “Hell yeah, Wren!” Raven slapped him on the back. “See, Jax? Better than a boy scout.”

 Jax tilted his head down and clapped politely. “Wonderful! I was worried we were going to freeze to death,” he said through a shit-eating grin in warm Louisiana air. “We gonna sing campfire songs?”

 “Oho,” said Raven, raising their eyebrows, “you bet your ass we are. Some of the stereotypes are true, you know.” They pulled the most compact guitar Jax had ever seen out of one of their bags – it was banged up and covered in worn-out stickers, but it was still beautiful in its own way.

 Jax perked up, intrigued. He hadn’t done anything musical in, well, a long time. It was easier now to focus on the life before him; he imagined he was still on that train, moving ever forward with a long line of pipe smoke in tow.

 “You sing, Jax?” Raven asked.

 “…I– “

 “You do now! Here, this one’s pretty easy to follow along with – most of the words are ‘hard.’ Like, the literal word ‘hard;’ the song is easy. You get it. Here we go!” They broke into a classic-sounding folk progression on the guitar. It sounded shockingly full from such a little instrument; they played it with spirit. “Chorus first! Join in when you can!”

It's a-hard and it's hard, ain't it hard
To love one that never did love you?
It's a-hard, and it's hard, ain't it hard, great God,
To love one that never will be true?

 Jax was hesitant at first, joining in quietly at an octave lower than he was comfortable. Before long, though, the trio were singing loudly together about the pain of unrequited love, ramblin’ out West, and hard-liquor places.

 “You’ve got a wonderful alto, Jax,” said Wren. “You fit right in.”

 Jax blushed. “I’ve never had much vocal talent, unlike you songbirds.”

 “Nonsense,” said Raven, “your voice is lovely. One thing, though,” they took a hit from the communal whiskey bottle. “It’s bad luck to swap out pronouns in folk songs. It disconnects you from the stories, and from those who wrote them. If Woody Guthrie can sing from the perspective of a woman, you can too.”

 Jax turned a shade red, but tried hard to take it in stride. “Pssh, I’m gonna need more whiskey, then,” he said, grabbing the bottle. Wait, that’s not what I meant to say. He blushed harder as he took a swig.

 “I sing better after we’ve killed the first bottle,” Wren boasted. “Give here, Raven – I play the next one!”

 The trio laughed, sang, and drank into the night as the spark-filled smoke rose into the clear, starry night above them. Jax let his muscles relax with the conversation; the whiskey loosened his tongue and his wrists as he let himself be taken in by the melodies of the greats. When she sang, she imagined harmonizing with them as though they were there around the campfire, a great lineage of artists and vagabonds imparting their decades of wisdom to one whose life had really just begun.

 Raven kept locking eyes with Jax. Their looks were intense, but kind. They carried a question, she realized. She didn’t know what it was, but she knew the answer. “Yes.”

 “Wren, can you hold down the fort?” asked Raven, not taking their eyes off Jax.

 “Better than a boy scout!” he replied cheerily.

 “I’ve got something to show you, Jax. Come on, it’s not far.” They stood up and reached a hand out.

 Fear. Why was she afraid? She looked into Raven’s eyes – they gave little away, but there was a softness in that deep brown. Fuck it, why not, she thought, drinking a last swig of whiskey. She stood and took their hand.
