Grocery Store Masquerade

Baby Center Stage - Chapter 1/9

The polyester smoothness of his dark uniform polo sat as lightly on Leeroy’s skin as his long, black, voluminous hair brushed against his cheeks – together they enveloped him in a shady shroud that not even the harsh fluorescent lights of the store could penetrate. It felt almost like a cloud, comfortable as a cocoon but for the starchy collar locked distractingly around his neck. The gentle whirring of the AC, the buzz of the lights, and the distant beep of the self-checkout were interrupted by the unmistakable whine of an approaching cart – a customer.

 Leeroy was tired. It was almost the end of a long shift, and after hours of polite small talk the smiling muscles in his cheeks ached nearly as loudly as his feet against the utterly worthless standing mat beneath him. The customer, though, mirrored him in this regard – she had a disheveled, 30-something tiredness about her with dark circles and unkept frizzy brown hair to match. Their eyes met as she began loading her items onto the belt, and Leeroy felt his cheeks click into a knowing little smile to match hers. It was the kind of reflexive smile that feels ancient and intrinsic and deeply human, a fundamentally different creature than the wide, toothy thing he employed hour after hour that day for the customers’ benefit.

 As the belt whirred and Leeroy began scanning her things, he struck up a conversation. It was the substance of small talk at first, but Leeroy was too tired to do the usual dance, to keep the usual distance, and he found that she was too. “Man, I just don’t get paper towel math,” he was saying, “twelve triple XL equals ninety-six? Who comes up with this stuff?”

 “I’m not convinced there’s such a thing as a regular roll anymore,” she smirked. “I admit I haven’t really done the math, but these seem like an okay deal and they’re what my mom always bought, so they’ve got my undying loyalty for better or worse.”

 “Good enough for me,” he said, grinning, “math is booooring! I’ve never really been good at it, and I have it on good authority that I probably shouldn’t bother trying.”

 “I guess I should double check my receipt when you’re done then, unless you tend to err in the ‘less money’ kinda way.” She giggled contagiously, and Leeroy joined in easily. Her expression softened before she continued, “And not to have a problem with authority, but don’t listen to people about what you ‘can’t do’. They told my daughter in first grade that she didn’t have a brain for math, but – and I love her for this, she won actual money from a math competition this year.”

 Leeroy smiled warmly, dipping his head slightly in acknowledgement. “You must be very proud.”

 “Always – my girls are everything to me,” she said with a simple earnestness. They didn’t talk for long, though Leeroy did find himself deliberately slowing the check-out process to enjoy the conversation. He let himself bask in this little social bond, let its authenticity manifest in him physically as he animated his tone and affect – light, melodic, and feminine. It felt really good. It felt right.

 It was only when she turned to leave that he remembered the presence of his coworker, Ted, who shot him a look as he placed the last bag in her cart. Leeroy was familiar with that kind of look – it was a rebuke. He had broken some kind of social rule, and Ted was staring him back in line. Leeroy could feel his heart sink. “What?” he asked, nervously.

 “That was pretty faggy, dude. Can’t believe I have to work with a fuckin queer,” he laughed, lending a false affability.

 Leeroy gulped as though to fill the pit in his stomach; his guard was down for a measly five minutes and Ted had seen right through him. He suddenly felt the lights beating down on him, burning through his dark hair and illuminating his stupid, blushing face. The store was nearly empty, but he felt all the colorful eyes of a Sunday rush on him, witnessing his failure to just be a normal fucking guy for once. He was thrust onto the stage – it was perform or die.

 “Pssh, can’t you pick up on sarcasm either, Ted? That bitch never caught on that I was making fun of her right to the end. Like I would care about paper towels or her stupid spawn.”

 Ted smiled – this was more like it. “I dunno dude, it’s a little sus! You better not come on to me, man.”

 “Oh, was that a Freudian slip, Ted? I bet you’d like that, wouldn’t you?” Leeroy smiled, tilting his head as he held eye contact tauntingly.

 “Haha, fuck off, dude.” Ted said, conclusively. “And like, literally fuck off, your shift’s over.”

 And so it was, five past eight. Finally. Leeroy signed out of his station and stepped off stage, blowing Ted a taunting kiss as he walked back to the break room for his things. Ted sent him off with a playful middle finger, thus completing the ritual – Leeroy was off the hook.

 As Leeroy’s feet tapped across the mostly-clean tile of the empty store, he briefly tuned into the fuzzy music coming over the PA that he’d gotten so good at ignoring when the phrase “US Army” caught his ear.

The phoenix burned but did not rise
Now half the body’s paralyzed
There’s no one left to criticize
I hope you let it go

 Leeroy chuckled quietly, his lips pursing with amusement. Veronica’s got the aux again. The somber tune definitely did fit the energy of the store at this hour better than the usual top 40, but he’s not sure their manager would agree.

 He passed her at the customer service center; she was buried deep in her phone, her shiny black nails clacking across the screen. She had her own shroud of curly purple hair that went well with her piercings and bracelets – if it weren’t for the mandated polo she’d look right at home at a punk concert. Jax ran a hand through his bangs reflexively – not quite as long.

 “Hey Ronnie, gonna play Bring Me to Life after this? You’ve got me all nostalgic for my middle school scene years,” he jeered at her.

 “I told you not to call me that, asshole,” she replied without looking up. There was a hint of playful comradery in her insult, but only a hint.

 “Yeesh! You women take everything so personally these days. Later, bitch!” He called back in a sing-song voice as he walked away grinning. He found himself hoping that she found it funny; he did kind of want to be her friend, but he couldn’t bring himself to interact with her without making those kinds of jokes. Stupid, Leeroy, you’re such an idiot, he chastised himself as he badged into the employee area.

 Once alone in the stuffy little back room, Leeroy felt an overwhelming wave of lethargy – was it lethargy? – wash over him, and he stepped into the dirty single-stall employee restroom. The light flickered green over the sink mirror as Leeroy turned the water on. It had been a long shift and he was physically exhausted, but that didn’t explain the hard pit still lodged in the base of his stomach. He realized, all at once, that he was struggling to breathe – the cardboard collar on his uniform was choking him as his face dewed with a cold sweat. He pulled it off over his head, his hair falling back messily into his eyes and shooting off in every direction.

 With shame, he wrangled the black Amazon sports bra off too, nearly ripping it with the force he used, and flung it onto the floor. What a stupid fucking idea, he thought, pulling his hands through his hair and hunching forward in an embarrassed self-embrace. It had felt… nice, wearing it that day, and realizing that sent a cold pang from his stomach to his toes. His face contorted into an awful grimace, and he stamped his foot against the sticky tiles.

 It was becoming hard to stand. Leeroy leaned forward against the sink, his pulse thumping loudly in his ears as his chest heaved frantically. What the fuck’s the matter with me? Ted was right, I really am a little fucking queer, aren’t I? A stupid, broken fuck who can’t stop wishing for the impossible, for the depraved. God, I-I really am sick. Several moments of racing thoughts passed as the light blinked feebly.

 “Nope, nuh-uh. Enough, Leeroy! I’m done with this bullshit! It’s just in my head. I’m normal. I’m NORMAL!” Leeroy shouted, slamming his fist down onto the tile wall like a hammer, jostling the mirror. He looked up and saw himself. The green light washed unkindly over him, accentuating every ridge, every pore, every scar on his forearm, every bony protrusion on his form. Too large, too small, misplaced, misshapen, and malformed. It was too much to swallow, and he choked out tears as he averted his eyes down into the sink again. “No, no, no, nonono… god…” he sobbed.

 When his breathing slowed Leeroy sat down onto the grimy floor with his hands on his knees. He stayed that way for what felt like an eternity, his mind looping over and over, but losing momentum with each revolution, drawing down to the center. He reached over and picked up the sports bra, twisting it tightly between his fingers. His mind arrayed all those confusing years, all those anguished nights, and all those unknowable feelings into a grand, shining constellation. Finally, she blinked and looked up, taking a deep breath. There was no denying it, she knew. “I’m…” she whispered, faltering. Transgender. Say it. “…transgender,” she whispered, tearing up again. “Fuck.”
