Baby Center Stage - Chapter 8/9

Raven sat tensely in the barren, bleach white room. Blood from their mouth was dripping slowly onto their shirt, but their hands were chained to the floor in front of them. The monotonous ticking of an analogue clock, the only object of real note in the room aside from the table and chairs, tried to lull them into a trance, but they fought against it. They knew that every second was a second Jax was at risk; police custody was probably the most dangerous place she could be. An agonizing hour passed – Raven shivered in the cold light.

 Finally, the door’s lock disengaged and a balding man in his late forties with a button-up and a badge entered. “Camilla? I’m Detective Lehrer – you can call me Grahm.”

 “Where is she; what have you done to her?” Raven demanded emphatically.

 “You mean your friend in the dress?” He asked, raising an eyebrow. “Do you have any idea what kind of person you’ve been travelling with?” He walked over and sat at the table between them and the door.

 A dark anger stirred in Raven. “If you’ve hurt her…”

 “He’s fine. Don’t worry. Can I get you some water? Do you need to use the restroom?”

 “Fuck you, ‘fine’!” They snapped back. “She sure didn’t look fine while a pig ground her face into the asphalt! I have to get her out of here; she can’t spend the night. Can we post bail?”

 “You’ll be arraigned in the morning, when the courts open. I… don’t think your friend will be able to leave,” he said quietly, scanning their face for a reaction.

 “We’ll find a way. She’s not safe in here,” Raven declared, meeting his gaze unflinchingly.

 A few beats passed before the detective let out a soft laugh. “You really don’t know, do you? Wow!” He laughed again, and Raven imagined making him swallow his own teeth. Their dark smile vanished quickly when he continued, “Your friend’s in for murder. Killed his own mother just a few days ago.”

 Raven went white. Could she have? Lying fucking pigs; they always have an angle. “Bullshit,” they said flatly.

 Grahm shrugged. “Suit yourself. I thought you should know what you’ve gotten yourself into.” He leaned forward. “I just want to be sure that you had nothing to do with it, you know? You have some petty stuff in your priors, but nothing like this. You wouldn’t want to be an accessory after the fact, would you?”

 Raven’s eyes narrowed. “Fuck you. Lawyer.”

 “Um, hey, Jax,” Ribbit said, raising a hand to get their attention as she approached. There had been mention of an adventure beginning soon, but nobody else had showed up yet. Jax turned to her, but their face was… different. Disaffected. Ribbit was a little taken aback – had their conversation really soured them that much? It had been so good, right until the end, hadn’t it? “Look, about yesterday… I’m sorry if I pried too hard, or made you feel uncomfortable, or…” her eyes fell to her hands. The bow was probably too far, she thought, I… shouldn’t have presumed.

 “What? What’re you talking about?” Jax asked, frowning. What? “Ohhh, you mean, pssh,” they broke into a forced laugh, “you know none of that was real, right?” Ribbit looked hard into their eyes, but they gave nothing away. A sinking feeling filled her gut – Was it really that bad? Her mind raced, trying to think of how to reply in a way Jax would respond to, but they continued, “What, that corny backstory out of a Hallmark movie? Tch, come on. Really got ya, though!”

 “Jax… it’s okay. You don’t have to be ashamed. You’re my friend, and I-” Jax shot her a look that made her stop: anger. A deep, hateful anger crossed the space between them. Ribbit felt tears begin to well up as Jax abruptly walked away.

 Raven leapt into Wren’s arms, and he scooped them into a tight embrace. Some of the others waiting in the dirty police station lobby stared at the strange duo, but they couldn’t care less. “Thank the stars you’re okay – I was so worried! I…” he sniffed and took a shaky breath. “I-is it true? Did he really-“

 “-she. I don’t know, Wren. They haven’t let me see her.” Raven pulled him in tighter. “Wren, they beat her right in front of me; they had us both pinned and they beat her anyway.”

 “Oh,” Wren let out a pained sigh. “Bastards. I’m so sorry, Raven.” He broke the embrace and held them steady by the shoulders, looking into their eyes. “We’ll get her out of there. Somehow.”

 Ribbit wandered the circus aimlessly. Without her friend, the cavernous rooms truly felt empty. It was a dead space – devoid of all life, all meaning. Caine did his best to make their adventures entertaining, but that’s really all they could ever be: entertainment. It was all dessert, all the time – nothing of any real substance. She was starving; she could feel it.

 She lit up a bit when she rounded a corner and saw Jax talking to Kaufmo in front of the comically large toy phone. From a distance, they seemed to be having fun; laughing, smiling, gesticulating. Maybe they’re in a better mood today. Maybe things will go back to normal between us. It’s possible. Please.

 Ribbit waved and started to walk over. Jax noticed, and even from a distance she could see the shift in their expression. It was immediate; like a switch. Before she could get within ear shot Jax was already walking in the other direction.

 “Hey, Kaufmo,” she offered weakly when she came up to him.

 “Oh, hey Ribbit,” he smiled. “Haven’t seen you as much lately – you missed the last adventure.”

 “Yeah,” she looked at her feet. “Do you… does Jax seem alright to you, do you think?” She glanced up at him sadly, and there was concern, but also surprise, in his eyes.

 “I- yeah? Seems okay to me. Why?”

 “Oh, no reason. Heh. I, uh, I’ll see you around.” She started to walk off, and was surprised that the sinking feeling in her gut didn’t bring tears to her eyes. Too empty, I guess.

 It was getting worse. She and Jax hadn’t spoken more than a couple words to each other in weeks. They were actively ignoring her contributions to group conversations, freezing her out. The others were noticing, maybe subconsciously, and Ribbit didn’t have the energy left to try to reforge her bonds with them. She was just, so tired. She was tired all the time, now.

 One more time. One more try. They’re still my friend. It just has to work once. Ribbit approached Jax, sitting alone against the towering digital ball in the middle of the floor. They didn’t even look up. “I’m, uh, gonna go hang out in the café.” She cracked a weak smile.

 Jax’s eyes darted up to her – there it was again: anger. Ribbit’s heart sunk. “…Okay? Why are you telling me this?”

 She wanted to cry, but there was nothing inside her to give. “I… don’t know.” Ribbit slinked away, and Jax resumed staring blankly at the wall.

 There were three loud knocks on the metal door, then a harsh buzz and a ker-chunk as the officer pushed it open. “Mateo – this is Ms. Weaver, your court-appointed council.” The lawyer walked in confidently and sized up her client. He was a young, Turkic boy with long black hair and a sullen look about him. He looked up at her shyly and did not speak, so she began, “Hello Leeroy, it’s nice to finally meet you.” He nodded very slightly. She looked over at the policeman, “thank you, officer, I’ll take it from here.”

 “Ma’am,” he said, nodding his head and exiting the room, closing the door behind him with a loud chunk.

 “May I join you?” she asked her client. He nodded, and she sat at the table. “Do you prefer Lee or Leeroy?”

 “Jax,” he said so softly she could barely hear him.

 She leaned in, “I’m sorry?”

 He cleared his throat. “Lee is fine.”

 “Alright, Lee. Again, my name is Ms. Weaver, and I’ve been working on your case for several days now.” She opened her briefcase and laid out an assortment of binders, notebooks, and folders onto the table. She opened a small wooden box and removed an expensive-looking fountain pen, which she began to scribble notes with. “We don’t have the full details from discovery yet, but the charge is second-degree murder of Sara Mateo, your mother. Now, I’m your lawyer, that means everything you tell me is confidential. It’s important that you’re completely honest with me so that I can defend you as effectively as I can. I’m not here to judge you.” She took a breath. “Lee, did you do it?”

 “I…” Lee felt the words catch in his throat. He took a shaky breath. “I d-didn’t mean to. W-we had an argument, and she hugged me at the end of it. I p-panicked…” Lee paused as tears began to run down his cheeks. “A-and I pushed her off. I didn’t mean to hurt her.”

 “I see,” said Ms. Weaver, scratching her pen against a notepad. “Well, Lee, I do have an offer from the prosecution to share with you.”

 Lee couldn’t pay attention – the lawyer’s voice faded into the background as he retreated into himself. 30 years. He… could be in here thirty years. His breathing became rapid, and he noticed that the lawyer had stopped talking. “Lee, are you alright? Do you want me to explain something?” Her head tilted with mild concern.

 “Jax,” she said at last. “I want you to call me Jax.”

 The snowy wind cut right through Ribbit – it’s like Caine never considered that humans might find a bitterly cold mountainside an unpleasant adventuring location. She acclimated fast, though – it mattered not at all. Hot, cold, hail, lava, boils or locusts – it never mattered. She was alone, and she was starving.

 After a half-hearted effort in some hallucinatory antics, Ribbit noticed Jax alone, again, looking out from an overhang. She inhaled, then exhaled, and felt something stronger than she’d been able to feel in days: anger. She was angry at Jax. Finally. As she walked over to them, she knew that this would be it; they would fix it here, or their relationship would become irreparable. She was scared. She was determined.

 “Hey.” A semblance of Raven’s voice crackled over the line. Jax hunched forward, cradling the plastic of the phone within the shroud of her hair. The prison lights beat down unrelentingly, but in here, it was dark. It was quiet.

 She exhaled. “Hey.” Raven nearly cried right then, just hearing her voice – through the distortion it was still soft and gentle; it was the lovely girl she knew.

 “Jax. Jax I’m so sorry. We should have never – I-I should have been more careful… I-“

 “It’s okay. I’m- It’s okay.” She took a shaky breath. “I did this to myself. You tried to help me – y-you did, help me.”

 “Jax…”

 “I… can’t believe she’s dead.” Tears were running down her cheeks, something she knew she had to hide. “B-but, meeting you and Wren on that train… I wouldn’t change that.” She sniffed. “I’m so glad I got to know you, Raven.”

 “Don’t. Don’t do that, Jax. This isn’t goodbye; we’re going to get you out of there. We’re going to think of something.” Raven’s desperation buzzed over the line, tingling on the back of Jax’s neck.

 “Oh, Raven,” she cried gently. “Raven it’s going to be thirty years. T-thirty years in a featureless box where I have to be… where I have to stay….”

 “…No, Jax, no…” Raven whispered.

 “Hey, Jax, can we talk?” Ribbit tapped the rabbit’s arm, and they spun around in annoyance, but paused for a fraction of a second in surprise when they saw the ferocity in the frog’s eyes. They recovered quickly, though.

 “Ugh, where is there for us to possibly talk about?”

 Ribbit’s anger surfaced. “I don’t know, just… whatever I did, whatever’s going on between us, can we just stop this? I miss talking to you!”

 Jax’s voice was low and flat. “We’re here. We’re talking. What else do you want from me?”

 “I just want us to be friends again!” she shouted.

 Jax reflected her anger back at her. “What? So you can pry into my personal life and hold it over my head?”

 Ribbit was surprised; she searched Jax’s face, and did see a hint of… something, but she wasn’t sure. “…Is that really what you think? Jax I wouldn’t do that to you!”

 “You did do that to me! Why does it matter who we were out there? We’re in here! Forever!” they roared. “Nothing matters anymore, and there’s nothing we can do! Why don’t you just leave me alone?”

 “This can’t be what you want!” Ribbit protested, “Jax, I saw you, you were happy! You can be happy!”

 “You really think you know me so well? You really think we’re such SUPER close great friends, that we share everything with each other?” Their voice was dripping with a dark irony.

 “Jax, we were friends! We were great friends! And I’m sorry for prying!” Ribbit began to recoil into herself as she went on.

 “Oh my GOD!” screamed Jax, flinging their hands into the air.

 “I don’t want this one stupid mistake to ruin everything we have! Everything we are!” There was a pleading in her screams, which were more and more saturated with raw emotion.

 “Why are you still fixated on that night? Nothing happened!” Jax yelled desperately, with terror in their eyes. “You keep reading into things when there’s NOTHING THERE!”

 “What are you talking about? Jax-“

 “You’re using me! You’re MANIPULATING ME!”

 “-Jax do NOT even START!”

 Their shouts blended together, trembling with a common throughline of hurt, frustration, and fear. The storm picked up around them, and roared louder as their shouts began to die down. By the end of it they were both gasping for frigid air, exhausted.

 “I-I’m sorry,” whispered Ribbit, with resignation. “I’ll leave you alone.”

 The cell was dark. Cold. The scratchy blanket was little more than a placebo; Jax lay shivering with a dull flecked ceiling above her. She drew in a shaky breath, and drew the fountain pen out of her sock. It had leaked considerably; her fingers became slick with the viscous black ink as she rotated it above her. No matter.

 She carefully positioned the semi-sharp nib against her wrist; a darkness leaked onto her skin and dripped down to the cot. Black. Red. She was shaking. Her fingers tightened around the pen and she pulled with all her might, snapping it in two as it sliced through her.

 Warm. She closed her eyes and pictured beautiful patterns – the colors, the shapes. She remembered the boxcar. The songs.

 Cold. She opened her eyes and the flecks on the ceiling danced for her, waltzing gently across the room. She remembered the dress. The embrace.

 “…fuck…” she uttered weakly. “I-I don’t want to go.”

 “Wait,” the word was lost in the snowstorm. Jax called louder, “Wait!” Still, nothing – the frog kept walking, hunched over in a melancholic shamble. “Ribbit, WAIT!” she screamed, running after her. Ribbit turned around, and startled as Jax fell to her knees and put her hands on her shoulders, looking up into her eyes. “Ribbit… Ribbit I’m so sorry,” she sobbed. “I’m such an idiot, I’m so stupid, I-I’m so sorry. I… don’t want you to go. Please….” she blubbered loudly.

 It was as though Ribbit’s heart had jump-started. Raw, unadulterated joy coursed from her core to her limbs, and she pulled Jax into a hug with all the strength she could muster. “Jax, thank you, oh my god thank you,” she sobbed.

 The snow could have melted around them; flowers could have bloomed beneath them on this barren mountain. The two held the timeless embrace of human souls reaching out across the void. Their hearts beat. Their lungs filled. They were alive. They were in love.
