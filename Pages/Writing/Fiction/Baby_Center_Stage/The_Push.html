The Push

Baby Center Stage - Chapter 2/9

“Late again, Leeroy?” his mother called from the living room as the door closed behind him. The little house was dark, save for glow of the TV and a couple thrifted lamps.

 Sigh, here we go. “Sorry, ma,” he said, “busy shift. Want something from the kitchen?”

 “Busy shift, right. Says the guy with an unskilled part-time job he’s not even good at. You don’t know what hard work is, you just complain and complain,” she snapped back.

 Well, guess that’s a no. He opened the fridge – it was pretty bare on the food front, but it did have a half bottle of wine, a six pack of Miller Lite, and a few White Claws. Leeroy let the coolness of the fridge waft over him – it felt nice in the hot night. He eyed the White Claws – they looked straight out of a commercial, glimmering in dew and beckoning. Black cherry; that sounds nice. His mom sounded annoyed enough already, though, and Leeroy knew that she wouldn’t let him walk in with such a girly drink without a comment that he really wasn’t in the mood for. He grabbed a beer.

 “What’s on?” he asked as he walked in and lay down on the couch perpendicular the TV, flashing a tired smile. His mom, sitting in her recliner, didn’t even look at him.

 “You know, your dad was working in an auto shop when he was your age. Paid for his school. But you’re too delicate for that, aren’t you Leeroy?” Leeroy said nothing. “You’re not even in school. It’s fucking typical, you know? Men these days – lazy, weak, and stupid. I’m so glad I don’t have a daughter who’d have to deal with you all.”

 Leeroy sat quietly. Maybe she was right – he wasn’t successful, or smart, or even particularly driven. He did feel guilty about it, though, and he didn’t need her salting the wound to feel it. Maybe he didn’t deserve to be in his dad’s life; maybe it was his fault he’d gone silent. He hated his dad for leaving, but he didn’t just hate him. His grip tightened around the bottle, and he took a long swig.

 The last thing she said called out to Leeroy – “I don’t have a daughter.” That was wrong, wasn’t it? This was so unfair. Maybe if she understood, maybe if she told her, she would leave her alone. Maybe her mom would understand the pain she felt, at least enough to give her space to breathe. She needed to breathe, and then she could think.

 Her heart pounded. She rolled the bottle between her fingers. The correction was on her tongue, but she couldn’t open her mouth. She had the words. They were right there. The TV switched back from commercial break, and they watched in silence. Leeroy stared far past the TV, its light a glowing static blur across her vision. Her mouth opened, and closed. She felt like she was perched atop a tall bridge, teetering. One step and there’s no going back. Did she hate herself this much? Did she love herself this much? Was she ready to permanently alter her relationship with her mom? Maybe when I’m older. Could she wait? Maybe it’s already too late. Around and around she spun, until she heard her mom speak again.

 “You know, I–“

 “You do!” she blurted, and felt every muscle in her body tense. Shit! Fuck!

 “What?” her mom cocked her head, confused.

 There was no going back now. She could barely believe her ears as she spoke. “You… do. H-have a daughter.” She chuckled nervously, staring straight ahead as a cold feeling washed over her.

 There was silence for a long, long minute. Eventually, her mom said, “what does that mean? You don’t mean, you?” She stared intently at her daughter, who turned her head to face her. Leeroy could just nod as tears welled up inside her and began to roll down her cheeks.

 Laughter. Her mom burst out laughing. It was a mean, jeering laugh, and it cut right through Leeroy. Shock, then confusion, then utter, utter shame. What the fuck. Oh fuck, what have I done? His cheeks turned bright red under the hot tears, and all he could do was stare as his mom cackled at his expense.

 “You?” she howled. “That is the most ridiculous thing I’ve ever heard in my fucking life, Leeroy! Ever since you were a kid, you’ve been a boy in the worst fucking way. Sloppy, careless, rude, all of it! And just fucking look at you – I mean, seriously? You’re taller than most men, and gangly as hell too. Is this why you’ve grown out that awful haircut? Leeroy that’s fucking sad.” She was slurring slightly as she yelled – Leeroy had never seen her this angry before. Every syllable stung; of course she was right. He was no girl; he never could be. He was just some sick degenerate who needed a lot of therapy. A failure of a man. An affront to the idea of womanhood. A fucking freak. He swung his legs off the couch and stood to leave.

 “Hey,” she said, standing to meet him. “I know you just said that to get back at me, to get my attention. Well, it worked.  Look at you, little baby center stage.” Leeroy winced. “But don’t worry, darling. You’ll always be my son.” She approached him, and he took a step back, but before he knew it she’d wrapped her arms around him in a tight hug.

 Oh fuck! Oh my god! I can’t breathe! Leeroy’s every nerve screamed. He was raw, angry, and frightened – and he couldn’t get a breath. He gulped for air, dropping the bottle with a clang. She squeezed tighter and he snapped, raising his hands up and shoving her with all his might.

 She fell, hard, smashing her head into the drywall which cratered beneath her before she crumpled to the floor. Leeroy took a step back, gasping, out of focus. After a few moments, he looked down to his mother – she was lying motionless, face down. His hands was still raised, and he turned them around. Did that just happen? He took a step toward his mother, and fresh tears rolled down his cheeks that he didn’t even feel coming. Was she dead? No, no she couldn’t be. He couldn’t bear knowing, he couldn’t bear what he’d done.

 He didn’t think. He just ran.
