Cursed From the Earth

Baby Center Stage - Chapter 3/9

Leeroy sucked hot air into his lungs as his heavy footfalls took him further into the dark night. The street was dotted with little lamps bleeding their light feebly into the void; he could follow those. No plan, no tomorrow, just the lights – driven like a moth.

 His lungs strained and his feet sent rings of static up his legs with each step. He had no idea how long nor how far he had run; he just knew that he had to stop. Stop and gasp and heave and cry. You’re pathetic. You look so stupid right now – this is the dumbest thing you’ve ever done, and that’s a competitive field. Suddenly, he noticed himself silhouetted against an old copse of trees before him – headlights, a car! Without thinking, he shot his thumb out to the side, still hunched over and gulping for air.

 The car slowed, then stopped. Shit, I’m a mess, he thought as he wiped his face with his sweaty shirt. Realizing it was futile after a few desperate moments, he spun around and strolled over to the burnt umber ’05 Corolla, looking like hell.

 The window rolled open. “Hey, are, uh… you okay?” The driver’s face showed genuine concern as he looked over his glasses.

 “Phew!” Leeroy exclaimed, raising his arms and moving to do some stretches he’d seen on an old exercise tape. “Good as I can be after that run! I was so close to a personal best, but I think I pushed it just a little too hard. You willing to help out little ol’ me? I promise I only smell a little bad.” He flashed his famous smile at the man, who still had a fair bit of concern in his eyes. He looks like a nerd… Leeroy surprised himself with the unkind thought. Whatever.

 “I, uh, sure. Here,” he popped open the passenger door. “Where are you going?”

 Leeroy froze for a moment – he hadn’t thought that far ahead. “Uhhh, I don’t really know the address, but it’s just a few miles that way” he gestured forward and hopped in, moving a couple books from the passenger seat onto the floor.

 “Oh, okay,” the man said softly. “Just tell me where to stop, I’ll take you as far as I can.” Leeroy could feel tears start to well up at the stranger’s kindness – it had been a very hard night. He couldn’t let him see that, though. He had to say something to distract himself.

 “Will do, old man, will do. Just don’t murder me on the way, promise?” Leeroy smiled big while the man chuckled uncomfortably, then they were off. The roads wound through the trees as they moved between towns, and more than once deer threatened to run out in front of them. Neither of them spoke for a long while; there was just the steady rumble of wheels on asphalt and the gentle sigh of the AC.

 Leeroy let his eyes wander around the car – no buck knives, rope, or duct tape, thankfully, but it was kind of a mess. There were some empty cans under his feet, as well as old receipts and parking lot tickets. The book he’d moved was The Queen’s Gambit, and there was a cracked CD case on top of it – some kind of album with green, swirling artwork.

 Finally, the man said, “Oh, uh, I’m Grant,” and held out his hand. Leeroy felt the same panic as when he’d been asked where he was going – there was an inexplicable yet overwhelming urge to not answer, but he knew he had to.

 “J-James,” he sputtered out after a suspiciously long pause. James. Why did I lie about that? Am I stupid? It at least didn’t feel like a bad name; it was different, and that was good. He shook Grant’s hand limply.

 “James, right,” said Grant, obviously choosing not to pry. They were getting back into a populated area, but decidedly more dilapidated. These were the eastern outskirts of Baton Rouge, an area that had certainly seen better days. There were several minutes of silence as the car weaved between the old structures.

 “Hey, kid, listen. You don’t know me, but you don’t look like you’re having a very good evening.” James looked over at him and saw only sincerity in his eyes. “I’m, uh, I don’t know what you’re going through, but I do know that, at your age, problems can feel insurmountable. I’m not saying your problems aren’t bad, but life is just so much bigger than anyone can imagine – it takes time and effort to really appreciate just how vast the world is, and just how many chances we really get. There will always be a space for you.” There was earnest care in his eyes, and a fatherly tenderness.

 James went white. “Pssh, sure dude. You got a publisher for that self-help book yet?” He cleared his throat uncomfortably. “This, uh, this is me,” he pointed at the first thing he saw: an abandoned concrete office building with ivy snaking up the walls. The shadow of a long-since removed logo was burned into the structure’s gray visage: “C&A”.

 “…This is you? A-are you sure?” Grant asked, clearly taken aback but slowing down anyway.

 “Yep! Tootles!” James yelled with a smile, flinging the car door open and walking briskly to the sagging chain-link fence surrounding the property.

 “What are the odds of that?” Grant said and sat quietly for a moment. He considered calling someone to check on the kid, but he couldn’t think of who would actually be helpful in this situation. Certainly not the cops. He wiped his moustache and shifted into drive – the kid’s fate was their own; all he could do was hope for the best.

 Once James saw the headlights disappear into the distance, he hopped the puny fence, nimble as a rabbit.

 James awoke on the ground, hungry and cold. His head was pounding, and it took him several seconds to realize first who he was, then where he was, and finally how he got there. When it all clicked back into place, a fresh stream of hot tears rolled sideways across his face and onto the dusty floor. Oh my god. This is real.

 Gray light filtered through the office window, reflecting off particles James had disturbed with his presence. He inhaled, drawing several of them in. Maybe I’ll be entitled to financial compensation in ten years, he mused darkly. Eventually, his stomach began to rumble, and he stood up.

 He didn’t have a plan, and that was a problem. Maybe my plan is to get run over by a fucking train he thought, then brushed it aside. Okay. What did he have? After a quick inventory, the answer was unsurprisingly “not much.” He had his wallet with about $60 cash, plus his ID, a $10 GameStop gift card, and his grocery store name badge. Aside from that he had a pen, two (2) paperclips, and a stick of gum. Hey, technically food. He popped it into his mouth – it tasted stale.

 He looked around – the office was surprisingly pretty fully furnished, almost like everyone had been raptured, or taken by aliens. Or raptured by divine aliens. “At least that’d be funny,” he muttered under his breath. Wow, I really must be losing it. It did occur to him, however, that some of the things left behind might be nominally valuable. And, at this point, that meant money for breakfast, at least. He began rummaging.

 Before long, James found himself opposite an old CRT monitor on the second floor. Next to it, a clunky headset lay temptingly on the desk. Huh. Not often you see VR equipment hooked up to this kind of setup. He stepped forward and picked it up – it was a heavy thing, and cold to the touch. As he rotated it in his hands he, just for a moment, thought he heard a read/write arm flicking about from within the computer tower, but that couldn’t be – it was clearly long-since off and ruined. He adjusted the headset’s straps, pushed his long hair out of the way, and slipped it over his head.

 First, nothing. Then, a faint whirring. Suddenly, flash of brilliant white light scorched his retinas and a hot pain slammed into his frontal lobe. He blinked rapidly, eyes watering.

 Darkness, the light was gone as quickly as it had appeared, and the whirring sputtered down. His eyes slowly adjusted to the cavernous room’s bright, saturated colors. Confused, Leeroy pulled the headset off and set it on the table. There were two yellow gloved hands in front of his face, as though holding something. He wasn’t sure what it was about it, but he didn’t feel like he should take the headset with him. He let down his hands, and the gloved hands fell also – how odd. “Uhh…”. He’d found a number of other likely pawnable things – he didn’t need a weird headset he couldn’t really explain, and which clearly didn’t work. A set of cartoon characters stared at him from the middle distance – a clown, a chess king piece, a frog, and a ragdoll. They looked, concerned? He moved his hand over the yellowed plastic of the computer tower, brushing the dust off – it was a beauty; what a shame it had gone to waste. “Okay…?” He tried to remove his headset but found that the gloved hands met the sides of his face, and he could feel them! The entire mental shape of his body was off, and he suddenly felt nauseous. With a shrug, Leeroy slung the bag he’d found over his shoulder and headed down the stairs, off to get food and figure out what he could possibly do with the remainder of his life. “Wait, wh- wait, what’s –“ he stammered as his cochlea sent him to the floor. The cartoon characters approached.
